    Installations

    As I grow older, more important decisions come my way. In the midst of these times, I struggle to find what path I should take. People tell me different viewpoints; some are facts, and some are just opinions. There’s so much to consider. How do I know what’s real and what’s just a myth?

    The words of my mentors, family, and friends convolute and leave me stranded in a world I don’t know how to navigate. I look around, and I don’t know what to focus on. The ones close by? The ones afar? The sharp ones? The hazy ones?

    This structure represents the convoluted situations of life. Parts of it are real. Parts of it are simply reflections that mimic reality.


    Pipe Dream

    Glass, Mirror, PVC Pipes

    20 x 20 x 15 in.

    As a frugal person, I couldn’t let myself throw away my old tie dye shirts, so I decide to repurpose them. Last year, I saw a friend drape a piece of clothing into many different styles. Inspired, I wanted to experiment with draping. But when I got 4 shirts in, I was suddenly drawn more toward the texture and pattern of my materials. With draping still in the back of mind, I sought out materials with varied texture to add dimension to this fashion piece. Inevitably, this piece became about the integration of numerous fabrics and textures in a modern tribal dress. My design is capable of shifting into various forms under different circumstances. It is malleable, adaptable, and fluid.

    Synergy

    Shirts, Cocoa Bag, Graduation Gown, Mesh, Tutu

    60 x 24 x 15 in.

    Several years ago, the trees in my backyard mysteriously began to die. Currently, the leaves on our orange tree are turning yellow, and the branches are turning white.

    These systematic events made my family wonder if something in the ground was killing our trees. After some thought, my father remembered the chemical leakage from a tank on the PG&E telephone pole that saturated our soil a couple years ago. I’m now afraid to eat the oranges from the tree, and my family is concerned about the long-term exposure to those chemicals affecting our health. At the time, PG&E quickly fixed the leakage but never warned us about any detrimental affects the chemicals may cause.

    I created this sculpture using leaves and branches from my yard. The fallen leaves and empty branches represent the damage caused by industrial progress, while the wheelchair symbolizes our family’s fear about our future health.

    Second-Hand Smoking: Suffocation

    Gas mask, Clear tubes, Black tubes, Glow sticks, Fog machine

    24 x 12 x 10 in.

    I used to put very little thought into what I ate. But, when I worked at a camp for people with mental or physical disabilities, I saw how careful many campers had to be with their diets. Their diets restricted them. For a camper with Type II Diabetes, eating too many fruits and vegetables could raise the blood sugar to dangerous levels. I saw people who were unable to eat fed through a G-tube. This sculpture represents how I will never look at food the same way again. I don’t take my unrestrictive diet for granted, and I’m aware that the simple act of eating can be both limiting and liberating.

    Food for Thought

    Plastic Straws, Forks, Spoons, Metal Utensils

    12 x 12 x 6 in.

    I find myself moving faster than ever in this evolving word. But, does this advancement come with a cost? As our modes of transportation become more comfortable, our concern for pollution, emission, and waste are often forgotten for the sake of leisure and luxury. From self-driving cars to jumbo jets, technology continuously advances.

    I selected a bicycle to represent our ability to travel quickly. Fingers, however, replace the brakes. The wearing down of the fingers as we travel from one place to the next reminds the viewer of the consequences of neglecting environmental issues in our daily activities.

    Underneath

    Wooden skewers, Tracing paper, LED lights, Silkscreen Fabric

    24 x 12 x 36 in.


    Much of who I truly am is hidden underneath the surface: invisible to those afar, but clear to those who choose to come near. At times, I appear different because it’s all based on their perspective, near or far, left or right, in darkness or in light.

    After reading Black Swan Green, a coming of age novel about a teenage boy with a stutter, I began to think more about speech impediments. Although learning the pronunciation for different languages, such as Spanish or Chinese, was challenging for me, it’s incomparable to the difficulties others have had to overcome due to speech impediments.

    This piece depicts my interpretation drawn from the insight I gained reading Black Swan Green, of what it feels like to battle a speech impediment. A cup is meant for drinking, but as one brings the cup towards one's lips, one is hindered. The fingers enter one's mouth and then proceed to one's throat. They grab hold and prevent sound from coming out even though one tries as hard as one can to fight it. It, the speech impediment, takes over, and the only thing others see are open lips with no sound emanating from them.

    Hangman

    Ceramic Cup, Polymer Clay

    5 x 5 x 4 in.

    Instead of exploring the definition of the given topic word, forge, I chose to explore an idea and concept the word embodies: artificiality, the notion of pretending to be something one is not. I wanted my execution and medium to reflect my opinion on deception in human behavior. With water soluble inks and paints, I painted words representing artificial qualities and aspects I have experienced, such as appearances, relationships, and compliments. I assembled these words on a mask to symbolize how people hide behind a wall of lies and fake impressions. Then, I sprayed and exposed the mask to water, allowing the inks and paints to dissolve. I wanted the words to blend and become indiscernible to illustrate how people can see through the falsities. The wearing down of the words into a blob of nothing shows how artificiality is meaningless and not genuine. It will lose its value over time, while authenticity will last.

    A Closer Look

    Photography, Digital Editing

    9 x 11 x 0.5 in.


    This photography book is centered around the idea of the beauty and power of defying stereotypes. I wanted to illustrate the uniqueness and beauty of seeing the world from a different point of view, not the common one everyone else sees.     In Hindsight

    Digital Drawing

    7 x 7 x 3.5 in.


    I was inspired to create this piece as I stared at a mirror in a small coffee shop. My former optic lessons in physics class flooded back into my memory as I pondered over lenses, mirrors, virtual images, and real images. These concepts led me to reflect on the illusion, deception, and perception in this world, which is what this book is about. Through simple images, my book transitions from illustrating how most people are deceived to how each person’s perception of the world is shaped by various factors, such as education, culture, and family. I chose to present my book in a circular format instead of using a traditional book binding technique because I wanted the presentation to add to my message. The circular shape of the book and the clear plastic the viewer initially looks through helps convey the idea that everyone sees the world through a different lens.

    Printmaking

    My experience with children with Autism sparked my curiosity about disabilities. As an artist, I felt that the best place to start my exploration and expression was through my own artwork.

    Knowing how my school embraces and supports students with a multitude of challenges, I decided to make Asperger’s syndrome, Down syndrome, sensory disabilities, and physical disabilities the inspiration of this piece. Each is represented by one of the four unique puzzle segments. I chose to use puzzle segments not only because it was the symbol for Autism, but also because it symbolizes the significance of each person. Like a puzzle piece, each person is unique, regardless of whether or not they have disabilities. Each person is equally important in completing the picture.

    A Different Ability

    Linoleum Block Prints, Digital Drawn Backgrounds

    16 x 22 in.

    Raised on a diet of noodles and rice, I’ve grown accustomed to Asian food. In fact, my favorite type of food is Asian food — Chinese, Taiwanese, Korean, Japanese, Vietnamese, and more. For this print, I created four linoleum blocks of four different Asian foods I loved — Taiwanese Xiao Long Bao, Korean Tofu Soup, Japanese Ramen, and Vietnamese Pho.

    Though different, the blocks intentionally resemble each other. Whether it is the way each block is printed or the design of each block, there are factors that bring unity into the piece. This unity represents the how different Asians communities can come together to appreciate and respect one another. Although it may start with a simple appreciation of different foods, I believe the cooperation in this Asian community, in all aspects, is growing.

    Urban Forest

    Linoleum Block Print, Chine-collé

    9 x 6.5 in.

    In a world of diverse cultures and peoples, I often find myself confused by all the different possibilities. This collage and drypoint piece symbolizes how life is full of options and there isn’t necessarily always a correct answer.

    From the skier to the plane, the images in this pieces represents different modes of transportation or movement. Whether it is traveling up in an elevator or swimming through a pool, the path we eventually choose will lead us in different directions. Up or down, left or right, the options are limitless. I’ve realized that the best choice will vary based on where I am, in water or in air, on snow or on land.

    Comfort Zone

    Screenprint, Fabric, Shoes

    Series: Societal Changes

    This series is centered around societal issues, particulary the topics that have prompted social activism and societal changes. The first image references the Feminism Movement. The pattern work and gradient from blue to red illustrates the gradual transition and increased acceptance of women in traditionally male roles. The second piece was inspired by the Black Lives Matter Movement. This monoprint expresses my hope that our country can and will fulfill its promises. The final piece focuses on the LGBTQIA+ community. The colored ornaments represent the diversity of this community. Yet, the glass and the shattered ornament highlight the difficulties these members continue to face in society.

    Series: Childhood to Adulthood

    This series focuses on the transition from childhood to adulthood. In particular, these artworks address the various issues and opportunities young adults are presented with. The first piece represents the loss of innocence as children are exposed to the darker aspects of human nature. The second piece illustrates the taste of freedom experienced by young adults. However, it also emphasizes that freedom comes with responsibility. The final piece highlights the increased power and voice an individual upon enterring adulthood.

    2D Artworks

    Lions are known to the be the king of the animal kingdom. Through this piece, I wanted to express how I believe other animals on the planet would respond to our actions. We’ve destroyed numerous habitats because of industrialization.

    This sketch depicts the roar of lion. I wanted the roar to symbolize the fury animals have because we are stripping them of natural resources and destroying their homes. The greenery that seeps in from the lion’s mane represents the life and energy the King embodies.

    I want to bring light to growing concerns of environmental and habitat protection. I want others to also recognize the consequences our actions have on other life in this world.

    Creeping Modernity

    Graphite, Color Pencil, Markers

    14 x 17 in.

    In our rush to consume earth's seemingly endless resources, there is barely enough time to consider the grim consequences. The way countries obtain their resources has become so routine and mundane it is almost like a drive through. First, they order; what resource am I going to strip from the earth today? Then, they pay; the mining and drilling begins. Finally, the order is ready; some people enjoy the resulting industrial growth, but others are left to deal with the consequences of land stripped of all natural resources. Earth will inevitably run out of resources. Then, the store has no choice but to close its drive through windows and doors.
